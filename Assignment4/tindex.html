The cultures of the Indian subcontinent are at least a couple of thousand years old. Through the ages, they progressed from oral traditions to the well codified classical literature and celebratory folk drama traditions to the sophisticated modern day movie and television industry. One thread that has connected the arts of the various eras and the regions are the rich performance music and dance arts.

History

Dance and dance drama were performed not just as pure entertainment. Dances were performed as:
  1. a celebration of a happy family event
  2. a celebration for the arrival of monsoons or for the harvesting of a good crop,
  3. as a tool in education - as stories from epics could be danced, enacted and burned in the minds of the young,
  4. as a form of worship - learnt and performed in its purest form to please the gods and the audience.

Kings and queens encouraged artists in their creativity and accorded an exalted position in their palaces to the performing artists. From the ancient literature, sculptures and paintings it is evident that rich and sophisticated dance traditions abounded everywhere.

Types of Dances

The dance traditions that were practised rigorously by professional artistes, often as a family tradition and occupation evolved into the Classical dance traditions of India. These dance systems are all supposed to follow the strict rules set by an ancient sage by name 'Bharata' whose book the 'Natyashastra'(the science of dance) is still used as the ultimate reference for classical dance. It is believed that Sage Bharata was divinely inspired to write his treatise, by the Hindu mythological god Shiva in his form as Nataraja, the Lord of Dance. The dance traditions of the Northern parts of India, which experienced an onslaught of invasions by the Persian and Middle Eastern nations evolved into the Kathak form of dance

The dances that were performed by the common folk evolved into the innumerable folk dance styles from the various regions of India. The energetic Bhangra dancers of Punjab, the sensous Lavani dance of Maharastra, Bihu the Spring Dance of Assam, the Garba dance of Gujarat performed during the 9-night festival of Navaratri, the Kolattam(stick) dances of the south are all performed at various regions and seasons by the lay person as a form of expression of joy or to give thanks for the bounty provided by the land and the divine almighty. One feature that stands out in any of these performances is - the color, shimmer and glitter that has a truly mesmerizing effect on the observers.

Classical

Despite being 2000 years old, the classical dances of India, regardless of the region where they evolved in, have a surprisingly single basis - a treatise on dance by the Sage ‘Bharata’ known as the ‘Natyashastra’. Historians are not sure whether it was a single person who is the author or a number of different people contributed to this work. This text which is widely referred to even to this day, discusses all aspects of stagecraft of dance- from the music, the makeup, the emotions and sentiments to be conveyed, the various gaits and poses, eye movements, hand movements to the rituals to precede and conclude a performance.

Common Features

All performances begin with a salutation to the elephant faced Lord Ganesha and an invocation to the dancing Lord Shiva. Shiva’s dance, according to the mythology brought the world into its current existence and symbolizes the eternal movement of all particles in the world. He dances on an evil monster which symbolizes the evil forces within us that we struggle with on a daily basis.

Makeup and jewellery: The eyes are lined with black so as to enhance the emotions expressed by the dancers. The faces may or may not be painted elaborately, but the fingertips of hands of both male and female dancers are decorated as are the feet. Women are likely to wear jewellery and flowers in their hair. Both male and female dancers wear jewellery around their necks and around their waists. A special type of padded bells called ‘ghungroo’ in Hindi are worn around the feet that works like a rhythmic musical instrument to the dance.

Region Form Picture Significance
South - Tamil Nadu Bharatanatyam Considered the purest form. Primarily was used in worship at temples, but has in the past 100 years or so been adapted to the entertainment industry
South - Andhra Pradesh Kuchipudi Similar to Bharatanatyam, but usually includes narration of a story from mythology. Dancers perform on plates and on inverted pots and it is known as the ‘tharangam’ form of the dance
South - the coastal regions of Kerala Mohiniattam Mohiniattam literally means the ‘dance of the enchantress’. The movements involve very rhythmic swaying and reminiscent of boats or palm trees moving in the wind
East - Orissa Odissi Form is similar to Bharatanatyam, but involves some characteristic bends in the torso and hand movements.
North Kathak A highly evolved aesthetic style which is a combination of the classical Indian and Persian influences. The characteristic twirls and rhythm based style was used equally to entertain as well as in traditional worship.
Northeast Manipuri One of the most elegant and graceful dance forms. The female dancers wear a characteristic, cylindrical skirt that moves rhythmically with the bobbing of the dancer’s movements

Region Form Picture Significance
South Kolattam(stick dance) Danced with rhythmic beating of the sticks and singing done by the dancers themselves
North - Punjab Bhangra An energetic dance performed by both male and female dancers to celebrate their bountiful harvest
East- Assam Bodo Assam is home to many types of festive and tribal dances to celebrate the festival of Bihu. Bodo is one such dance form
West Garba A very graceful dance performed during the 9 day Navaratri festival. The dance starts at a slow pace and picks up to a fast rhythm. The female dancers wear very colorful skirts that twirl and create beautiful patterns.
