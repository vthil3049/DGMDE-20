The Scope of Indian Dances

The Classical dances

Despite being 2000 years old, the classical dances of India, regardless of the region where they evolved in, have a surprisingly single basis - a treatise on dance by the Sage ‘Bharata’ known as the ‘Natyashastra’. Historians are not sure whether it was a single person who is the author or a number of different people contributed to this work. This text which is widely referred to even to this day, discusses all aspects of stagecraft of dance- from the music, the makeup, the emotions and sentiments to be conveyed, the various gaits and poses, eye movements, hand movements to the rituals to precede and conclude a performance.

Common Features

All performances begin with a salutation to the elephant faced Lord Ganesha and an invocation to the dancing Lord Shiva. Shiva’s dance, according to the mythology brought the world into its current existence and symbolizes the eternal movement of all particles in the world. He dances on an evil monster which symbolizes the evil forces within us that we struggle with on a daily basis.

Makeup and jewellery: The eyes are lined with black so as to enhance the emotions expressed by the dancers. The faces may or may not be painted elaborately, but the fingertips of hands of both male and female dancers are decorated as are the feet. Women are likely to wear jewellery and flowers in their hair. Both male and female dancers wear jewellery around their necks and around their waists. A special type of padded bells called ‘ghungroo’ in Hindi are worn around the feet that works like a rhythmic musical instrument to the dance.
